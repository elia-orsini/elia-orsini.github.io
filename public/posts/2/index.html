The Glass Bead Game

October 9, 2021 · 2 min

The Glass Bead Game by Herman Hesse is a biography written by a historian in the year 2400 about an elite of people which decide to live alienated from society to study and preserve all the culture accumulated in the centuries.

Even though it is a fictional book, it feels more real than anything. Everything seems just like a metaphor where the author’s aim is talking to you about his own path from great expectations and convictions about the meaning of the world to an existential crisis where every certainty about your life vanishes. And Herman Hesse has definitely something to say about this from his experience. With the outbreak of the First World War, Hesse’s self-exile to Switzerland coincided with the death of his father, the serious illness of his son Martin and his wife’s schizophrenia episodes.

It is not a philosophy book but it is certainly a philosophical book. As great novels should do, It makes you wonder and wonder multiple times about your own life. It gets into you very easily making you meditate about your past decisions and your future ones.

It is introspective and it might easily induce radical changes. It’s a dangerous book. Dangerous because it instills in your mind crazy thoughts about isolation, obsessive study and the overall scope of your existence. It is a very vivid and desperate reading, where you see in the beginning a hopeful protagonist holding a set of solid convictions to then slowly throw away each one of them until meaningless is the only thing that remains.

I will probably read this novel again. I feel like it is so dense with meaning that reading it just once is not enough. I still need to make sense of the finale.
